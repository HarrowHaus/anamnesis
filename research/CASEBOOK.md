### The Starbucks Siren (Twin-Tailed Melusine)
- **Surface:** A crowned, smiling female figure inside a green disc, with long flowing hair and (in early versions) two splayed fish-tails held in her hands.
- **The decode:** In the symbol-decoding tradition the figure is read as the twin-tailed Siren/Melusine — an alchemical emblem of the dual nature (water/body and soul) and a "lure" figure who draws the public in. The two tails are read as the alchemical union of opposites, and the crowned water-spirit as a survival of the goddess/Lilith archetype (beautiful above, serpentine below) appearing on a mass-market object.
- **Symbol lineage:** The twin-tailed mermaid is documented in medieval European art (a 7th-century Byzantine/Italian motif, carvings such as San Michele in Pavia, and the Hortus Sanitatis woodcut of 1491). [Mermaids of Earth](https://mermaidsofearth.com/starbucks-mermaid/) Starbucks itself says the logo derives from a 16th-century twin-tailed siren woodcut; [Gotmedieval](https://www.gotmedieval.com/2010/08/the-other-starbucks-mermaid-cover-up.html) the figure was specifically tied to "Melusine" after Sabine Baring-Gould's 1866 study. [Shimajournal](https://www.shimajournal.org/article/10.21463/shima.190) Melusine is the serpent-tailed heroine of Jean d'Arras's c.1393 Roman de Mélusine, ancestress-legend of the House of Lusignan. The image J.E. Cirlot catalogued in A Dictionary of Symbols labels it a 15th-century twin-tailed siren. [Gotmedieval](https://www.gotmedieval.com/2010/08/the-other-starbucks-mermaid-cover-up.html)
- **Who teaches this decode:** Symbol-dictionary readers in the Maxwell/Tsarion tradition; the alchemical reading is summarized at the Symbol Dictionary and discussed in Shima journal's scholarly history (Allison 2023).

### Bernays's "Torches of Freedom" Campaign
- **Surface:** On Easter Sunday, March 31, 1929, roughly ten debutantes lit Lucky Strike cigarettes while marching in New York's Easter Parade on Fifth Avenue; the New York Times ran it front-page on April 1, 1929: "Group of Girls Puff at Cigarettes as a Gesture of 'Freedom'."
- **The decode:** Read in the symbol-decoding tradition as the deliberate grafting of the goddess/torch "light-bearer" emblem onto a consumer product — the cigarette reframed as a "torch of freedom" so that the lineage of Libertas/Columbia's illuminating torch is mobilized to sell tobacco and engineer mass behavior. The campaign is the textbook case of symbol-engineering: attaching an ancient liberation emblem to a product.
- **Symbol lineage:** Documented: Edward Bernays (Freud's nephew, "father of public relations") staged the event for George Washington Hill of the American Tobacco Company; the phrase came from psychoanalyst A. A. Brill, who told Bernays: "Cigarettes, which are equated with men, become torches of freedom." The torch is the same emblem of liberty carried by Libertas/Columbia/the Statue of Liberty. The campaign tracked a sharp rise in female smoking: women bought 5% of cigarettes sold in 1923, 12% by 1929, 18.1% by 1935, peaking at 33.3% in 1965. The 1929 stunt and its myth are documented by Bernays's own Biography of an Idea and by scholarship (Murphree, American Journalism, 2015). [History Today](https://www.historytoday.com/miscellanies/original-influencer)

### The Fasces (Bonus — Civic/State Branding)
- **Surface:** A bundle of wooden rods bound around an axe; in the U.S. House chamber two large bronze fasces flank the flag behind the rostrum, [The New York Review of Books](https://www.nybooks.com/articles/2023/11/23/authoritarianisms-emblem-the-fasces-t-corey-brennan/) and fasces appear on Lincoln's armrests at the Lincoln Memorial and on the reverse of the 1916–1945 "Mercury" dime.
- **The decode:** Read in the symbol-decoding tradition as the Roman emblem of state power over life and death — the magistrate's authority to scourge (rods) and behead (axe) — placed openly in the chambers and monuments of government as a declaration of imperium hidden in plain sight.
- **Symbol lineage:** Documented: the fasces originated with the Etruscans and passed to Rome, where it symbolized a magistrate's imperium and was carried by lictors; [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fasces) "the rods represent the power of the state to beat you, the axe its power to behead you" (Smith's Dictionary), and a 7th-century BCE miniature iron set from an Etruscan tomb at Vetulonia confirms the Etruscan origin (Britannica). In the U.S. it appears as two bronze fasces flanking the flag in the House chamber [Architect of the Capitol](https://www.aoc.gov/explore-capitol-campus/buildings-grounds/capitol-building/house-wing/house-chamber) (Architect of the Capitol: "An American flag occupies the center and is flanked by two bronze fasces"), on the armrests of Daniel Chester French's Lincoln [U.S. National Park Service](https://www.nps.gov/articles/secret-symbol-of-the-lincoln-memorial.htm) (National Park Service: "French's 'Abraham Lincoln' prominently depicts fasces on the ends of the armrests"), and on the Mercury-dime reverse (1916–45, replaced by a torch on the 1946 FDR dime).
- **Who teaches this decode:** The "power over life and death / imperium" reading is taught across the symbol-decoding tradition; the Roman-lictor origin and U.S. placements are documented by the Architect of the Capitol, the U.S. House History office, and the National Park Service.
