## Details

### c. 45,000+ years ago — Paleolithic symbol-making (earliest image-making)
- **What happened:** The oldest securely dated figurative cave paintings yet found are in the Maros-Pangkep karst of Sulawesi, Indonesia. A painting of a Sulawesi warty pig in Leang Tedongnge has been dated to at least 45,500 years old, and a narrative scene at Leang Karampuang (a pig and three human-bird hybrid figures) has been dated to at least 51,200 years old in a 2024 study led by Maxime Aubert. In Europe, a red disk in El Castillo cave, Spain, is at least 40,800 years old. Non-figurative red symbols in Spanish caves (Maltravieso, Ardales, La Pasiega) have been dated by some researchers to ~64,000 years, implying Neanderthal authorship.
- **Why it matters (image/symbol-control angle):** This is the baseline — the human capacity to make symbolic images that carry meaning beyond the literal. Everything downstream (banning, standardizing, weaponizing images) presupposes this capacity. The deep caves' restricted, hard-to-reach locations suggest images were already tied to ritual and controlled access.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Maxime Aubert and the Griffith University team; hand stencils; animal figures; the El Castillo red disk.
- **Sources:**

### c. 1353–1336 BCE — Akhenaten's monotheistic image reform and his later erasure (early state image-control + damnatio)
- **What happened:** Pharaoh Amenhotep IV changed his name to Akhenaten ("Effective for the Aten"), elevated the sun-disk Aten as sole deity, founded a new capital (Akhetaten/Amarna), and imposed a new artistic style. He ordered references to the god Amun chipped away. After his death (~1336 BCE), his successors reversed the reform; his son Tutankhaten became Tutankhamun ("Living Image of Amun"). Later, Horemheb led a campaign to destroy Akhenaten's monuments, deface his images, and omit him from king lists — a classic case of what would later be called *damnatio memoriae*.
- **Why it matters:** This is one of the earliest documented cases of state-directed image reform AND state-directed image erasure — control of the visual record as control of religion, politics, and memory itself.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Akhenaten, Nefertiti, Tutankhamun, Horemheb; the Aten sun-disk with rays ending in hands; Amun.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Damnatio memoriae" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damnatio_memoriae
  2. World History Edu, "What was the Amarna Period?" — https://worldhistoryedu.com/what-was-the-amarna-period/
  3. The Collector, "Why Ancient Egyptians Tried to Erase the Memory of Some Pharaohs" — https://www.thecollector.com/damnatio-memoriae-ancient-egypt/

### c. 13th–6th centuries BCE (text crystallized later) — Hebrew aniconism / the Second Commandment
- **What happened:** The Hebrew Bible prohibits making "graven images." Exodus 20:4–5 commands: "You shall not make for yourself a graven image or any likeness which is in the heavens above…" Aniconism gained practical prominence in Judahite religion during the late 8th–7th centuries BCE through reforms attributed to Kings Hezekiah and Josiah, who destroyed cultic idols and centralized worship in Jerusalem.
- **Why it matters:** The Second Commandment is the foundational Western textual basis for image prohibition. It is repeatedly invoked across history — by Byzantine iconoclasts, Protestant reformers, and Islamic aniconists — as divine warrant for destroying or banning images.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Moses; YHWH; the Golden Calf episode (Exodus 32); Kings Hezekiah and Josiah; Asherah poles.
- **Sources:**

### c. 380 BCE — Plato banishes the poets (Republic, Books II–III and X)
- **What happened:** In the Republic, Plato's Socrates argues that imitative poetry (mimesis) should be censored or banished from the ideal city. Books II–III call for censoring poets' false and harmful tales about gods and men; Book III bans the mimetic genres of tragedy and comedy; Book X (607) extends the critique, even expelling Homer as "the first teacher" of tragic poetry, on the grounds that imitation is "two steps removed" from reality and corrupts the soul.
- **Why it matters:** This is the founding text of Western image/representation censorship — a philosophical argument that mimetic representations are dangerous because they bypass reason and shape the soul. Plato has been called the "founding father of Western literary censorship."
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Plato, Socrates, Homer; the concept of *mimesis*; the "noble lie."
- **Sources:**
  1. M.F. Burnyeat, "Art and Mimesis in Plato's Republic," London Review of Books — https://www.lrb.co.uk/the-paper/v20/n10/m.f.-burnyeat/art-and-mimesis-in-plato-s-republic

### 27 BCE–14 CE — Augustus, the imperial cult, and Roman coinage as propaganda
- **What happened:** Augustus systematized the use of state imagery: statues placed him in temples of the imperial cult; coins called him "son of (a) god" (after Caesar's deification); and a vast building and portrait program projected legitimacy. According to the Roman Imperial Coinage catalogue, some 550 distinct coin types were issued between Actium (31 BCE) and Augustus's death (14 CE). Statues like the Augustus of Prima Porta functioned as imperial propaganda distributed empire-wide.
- **Why it matters:** Roman state imagery is the archetype of centralized, standardized image-propaganda — coins and statues as mass media that normalized one-man rule and divine kingship across a diverse empire.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Augustus, Julius Caesar, Livia; the denarius and aureus; the imperial cult; *Pax*; the laurel.
- **Sources:**

### 1st–4th centuries CE — Roman damnatio memoriae and standardization of conquered imagery
- **What happened:** Rome practiced *damnatio memoriae* — the formal condemnation of memory — defacing statues, erasing inscriptions, and removing disgraced emperors (e.g., Domitian, Geta) from the public record. Conquered peoples' deities and imagery were frequently absorbed, restyled, or subordinated to Roman forms (interpretatio romana), and local cults were folded into the imperial framework.
- **Why it matters:** Damnatio memoriae is the most explicit Western institutionalization of image-erasure as political punishment; the absorption/standardization of conquered imagery shows image-control as a tool of empire-building and cultural domination.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Emperors subjected to damnatio (Domitian, Geta, Commodus, Nero); chiseled inscriptions; defaced statues.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Damnatio memoriae" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damnatio_memoriae
  2. Ancient Origins, "Damnatio memoriae: Purging People from Public Memory" — https://www.ancient-origins.net/history/damnation-memory-and-efforts-erase-condemned-history-002331

### 726–787 CE (First Iconoclasm) — Byzantine Iconoclasm begins
- **What happened:** According to the traditional account, Byzantine Emperor Leo III ordered the removal of an image of Christ from the Chalke Gate of the imperial palace in Constantinople around 726–730, replacing it with a cross, and banned the veneration of religious icons as idolatry. A submarine volcanic eruption near Thera in 726 was interpreted as divine judgment. His son Constantine V made the suppression official at the Council of Hieria [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Council_of_Nicaea) (754), which 338 bishops attended. Widespread destruction of icons and persecution of iconodules (icon-venerators) followed.
- **Why it matters:** The most sustained state campaign of religious image-destruction in Christian history — an emperor using the Second Commandment to assert control over the Church's visual culture, destroying a vast amount of early Byzantine art.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Leo III, Constantine V; the Chalke Gate icon of Christ; the cross (as aniconic substitute); John of Damascus (icon defender).
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Byzantine Iconoclasm" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Iconoclasm
  2. Britannica, "Leo III" — https://www.britannica.com/biography/Leo-III
  3. Byzantine Museum Thessaloniki, "Iconoclasm" — https://byzantine-museum.gr/iconoclasm/

### 787 CE — Second Council of Nicaea restores icon veneration
- **What happened:** Empress Irene, regent for Constantine VI, convened the Seventh Ecumenical Council at Nicaea. It opened 24 September 787 at Hagia Sophia, numbered about 350 members (308 bishops signed), and was presided over by Patriarch Tarasios [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Council_of_Nicaea) with papal legates of Pope Hadrian I present. It reversed the Council of Hieria, decreeing that icons deserve honorific veneration (*proskynesis*) but not true adoration (*latreia*), which is reserved for God, and that "the honor given to an icon passes through to its subject."
- **Why it matters:** A landmark act of image-*standardization* rather than suppression — defining the theologically permitted use and degree of veneration of images, a ruling still authoritative in Orthodox and Catholic churches. (Iconoclasm revived 814–842 under Leo V and others before final restoration in 843, the "Triumph of Orthodoxy.")
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Empress Irene, Patriarch Tarasios, Pope Hadrian I; the icon; the "Triumph of Orthodoxy" feast.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Second Council of Nicaea" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Council_of_Nicaea
  2. Britannica, "Second Council of Nicaea (787)" — https://www.britannica.com/event/Second-Council-of-Nicaea-787
  3. Papal Encyclicals, "Second Council of Nicaea – 787 A.D." — https://www.papalencyclicals.net/councils/ecum07.htm

### 7th–9th centuries CE onward — Islamic aniconism
- **What happened:** Aniconism — the avoidance of images of sentient beings — developed in Islamic religious art. The Quran condemns idolatry but does not explicitly prohibit depicting living beings; the more explicit prohibitions are in the hadith (notably the Sahih al-Bukhari and Sahih Muslim collections), which warn image-makers they will be challenged to "breathe life" into their creations and punished on the Day of Judgment. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aniconism_in_Islam) Sunni exegetes from the 9th century onward increasingly read these as categorical prohibitions, [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aniconism_in_Islam) fostering calligraphy, geometric, and arabesque art.
- **Why it matters:** A major, enduring tradition of religious image-control rooted in the belief that creation of living forms is God's prerogative — shaping the entire visual culture of much of the Islamic world toward non-figurative forms.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** The Prophet Muhammad (depicted in some Persian/Mughal manuscripts, often with face veiled); *musawwir* (God as "maker of forms"); calligraphy; arabesque.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Aniconism in Islam" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aniconism_in_Islam
  2. Academia.edu, "Aniconism in Islam" — https://www.academia.edu/87436561/ANICONISM_IN_ISLAM

### c. 1440 — Gutenberg's printing press and the mechanical reproduction of images
- **What happened:** Around 1440 in Mainz, Johannes Gutenberg invented the movable-type printing press. A single press could produce up to ~3,600 pages per day versus a few dozen by hand. By 1500, per Wikipedia's "Global spread of the printing press," an estimated twenty million books had been printed for a European population of perhaps seventy million (Britannica gives a more conservative figure of more than 9,000,000 books by 1500). Images were reproduced primarily via woodcut (and later engraving); printed anatomical plates, star charts, and broadsides (illustrated single-sheet "posters") could now be replicated identically and at scale.
- **Why it matters:** The press transformed image-control from a question of unique objects to one of mass reproduction — enabling standardized iconography, propaganda broadsheets, and the wide circulation of both religious and political images. It set the stage for the Reformation's pamphlet-and-woodcut war.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Johannes Gutenberg; the woodcut; the broadside/broadsheet; the 42-line Bible (1455).
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Printing press" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing_press
  2. Wikipedia, "Global spread of the printing press" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_spread_of_the_printing_press
  3. Reformation21, "The Printing Press and the Protestant Reformation" — https://reformation21.org/the-printing-press-and-the-protestant-reformation-php/

### 1522–1566 — Protestant Reformation iconoclasm (the Beeldenstorm)
- **What happened:** Reformed (Calvinist) leaders — Andreas Karlstadt, Huldrych Zwingli, and John Calvin — urged the removal of religious images, invoking the Second Commandment's ban on idolatry. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iconoclasm) (Lutherans were more tolerant of images.) Iconoclastic riots occurred in Zürich (1523), Copenhagen (1530), Münster (1534), Geneva (1535), and Scotland (1559). The largest wave, the **Beeldenstorm** ("statue storm"), swept the Low Countries in summer 1566, beginning 10 August 1566 at Steenvoorde after a sermon by Sebastiaan Matte; per Wikipedia ("Beeldenstorm"), organized bands of iconoclasts numbering around 3,000 ravaged approximately 400 churches in the region alone, and in Ghent the cathedral, eight churches, 25 monasteries/convents, 10 hospitals, and seven chapels were wrecked.
- **Why it matters:** The most destructive wave of European Christian iconoclasm since Byzantium — popular and theological image-destruction that became inseparable from the political revolt against Catholic Habsburg rule.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Karlstadt, Zwingli, Calvin, Sebastiaan Matte; smashed statues, altars, and stained glass; the Beeldenstorm.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Beeldenstorm" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beeldenstorm
  2. Wikipedia, "Iconoclasm" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iconoclasm

### 1547–1559 and 1640s — English Reformation iconoclasm and Puritan iconoclasm
- **What happened:** English image-destruction came in waves. It began with the Dissolution of the Monasteries (1530s–1540s) under Henry VIII and Thomas Cromwell's 1538 injunctions. It intensified under Edward VI: the Royal Injunctions of 1547 set off a wave of iconoclasm in autumn 1547, and a 1549 decree ordered citizens to "deface and destroy" images; injunctions followed in 1547, 1548, and 1549. Catholic restoration under Mary I (1553–1558) reversed it; the Elizabethan Settlement (from 1559) re-established Protestantism. A second major wave came during the 1640s English Civil War under Puritan authority (e.g., William Dowsing's commissioned destruction in East Anglia).
- **Why it matters:** A case of *government-sanctioned* (rather than mob) iconoclasm, distinguished by its characteristic disfigurement — striking off noses, faces, and hands of statues — and by whitewashing church walls to teach "through absence," symbolizing the shift to *sola scriptura*.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Henry VIII, Edward VI, Thomas Cranmer, Thomas Cromwell; the Royal Injunctions; whitewashed walls; later, William Dowsing.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Edwardian Reformation" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwardian_Reformation
  2. Maidens and Manuscripts, "Iconoclasm in 16th Century Western Europe" — https://maidensandmanuscripts.com/2021/04/01/iconoclasm-in-16th-century-western-europe/

### 1563 — The Council of Trent's Decree on Sacred Images (Counter-Reformation)
- **What happened:** At its 25th session (3–4 December 1563), the Council of Trent issued the "Decree on the Invocation, Veneration, and Relics of Saints, and on Sacred Images." Reaffirming Nicaea II, it confirmed that sacred images are legitimate and to be venerated (the honor passing to the prototype, not the image itself), while imposing new controls: bishops were to police images for doctrinal correctness and decorum, banning lasciviousness and "profane" elements. Cardinals Charles Borromeo and Gabriele Paleotti (whose 1582 manual codified the rules) drove the reform.
- **Why it matters:** The Catholic answer to Protestant iconoclasm — not abandoning images but *regulating and standardizing* them, channeling religious art toward the emotionally persuasive Baroque while subjecting it to ecclesiastical approval and censorship.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Pope Pius IV, Charles Borromeo, Gabriele Paleotti; the Baroque; Michelangelo's *Last Judgment* (later partly painted over); the *Index Librorum Prohibitorum*.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Council of Trent" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Council_of_Trent
  2. "Sacred Images and the Council of Trent" (decree text) — https://idlespeculations-terryprest.blogspot.com/2007/03/sacred-images-and-council-of-trent.html
  3. Britannica, "Council of Trent" — https://www.britannica.com/event/Council-of-Trent

### 1791 & 1795 — Volney and Dupuis systematize astrotheology (publication events)
- **What happened:** Two French Enlightenment authors published works arguing that religions are coded representations of astronomical phenomena. Constantin-François Volney published *Les Ruines, ou Méditation sur les révolutions des empires* in 1791, [Wikipedia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Ruines_(Volney)) which included an astronomical interpretation of religions and described Christianity as an "allegorical cult of the sun." [Wikipedia](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Ruines_(Volney)) Charles-François Dupuis published the multi-volume *Origine de tous les cultes, ou Religion universelle* in 1795 (with an illustrated atlas of zodiacal tables), arguing through comparative analysis that gods and myths are allegories of the sun, moon, and constellations, and that the life of Jesus is a "solar fable." Dupuis presented the work to fellow deputies on 7 September 1795; the Church placed it on the Index in 1827.
- **Why it matters:** This is the systematized origin of modern "astrotheology" — the *theory* that religious imagery and symbols encode astronomy. These are interpretive claims by Volney and Dupuis (the mythicist thesis on Christianity), not established historical fact; what is factual here is the publication of these influential books and their role in Enlightenment anti-religious propaganda.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Volney, Dupuis (also a founder of the Institut National); the zodiac; Sol Invictus; the "solar Christ" thesis.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia (FR), "Origine de tous les cultes, ou Religion universelle" — https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origine_de_tous_les_cultes,_ou_Religion_universelle
  2. Wikipedia, "Charles-François Dupuis" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles-Fran%C3%A7ois_Dupuis
  3. Wikipedia (FR), "Les Ruines (Volney)" — https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les_Ruines_(Volney)

### 1796 onward — Lithography and the rise of mass poster/advertising imagery
- **What happened:** Alois Senefelder invented lithography around 1796–1798, enabling cheap, high-volume printing of images (and, with chromolithography, color). Through the 19th century this fueled an explosion of mass-produced posters, advertising imagery, illustrated newspapers, and commercial iconography, especially the large color advertising poster popularized in late-19th-century Paris.
- **Why it matters:** Lithography put commercial and political imagery into the streets at scale, shifting image-power toward commerce and advertising — the technological precondition for the 20th-century "engineering of consent."
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Alois Senefelder; the advertising poster; chromolithography; Toulouse-Lautrec and the Belle Époque poster.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "Lithography" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithography

### 1839 — The birth of photography
- **What happened:** Louis Daguerre's daguerreotype process was announced to the public in 1839 (the French government presented it as a "gift to the world" in August 1839), with Henry Fox Talbot's competing negative-positive process announced the same year. Photography introduced the mechanically captured, apparently objective image — and, almost immediately, photographic manipulation and staged/propaganda photography.
- **Why it matters:** Photography created the powerful illusion of the image as objective evidence, which made photographic propaganda and later doctored/retouched photographs (e.g., Stalinist photo-erasures) especially potent tools of control.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Louis Daguerre, Henry Fox Talbot; the daguerreotype; photographic retouching/airbrushing.
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "History of photography" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_photography

### 1920s — Edward Bernays industrializes consent
- **What happened:** Edward Bernays (1891–1995), Sigmund Freud's nephew and a veteran of the U.S. Committee on Public Information (WWI propaganda), applied psychoanalytic and crowd-psychology ideas to peacetime persuasion. His books *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923) and *Propaganda* (1928) founded modern public relations and theorized the "engineering of consent" (a phrase he formalized in a 1947 essay). His most famous campaign — the "Torches of Freedom" stunt branding women's cigarettes as feminist liberation — was staged on Easter Sunday, March 31, 1929 on Fifth Avenue, and The New York Times ran it front-page on April 1, 1929 as "Group of Girls Puff at Cigarettes as a Gesture of 'Freedom'" (per History Today, "Edward Bernays: The Original Influencer"). In *Propaganda* he wrote: "The conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses is an important element in democratic society." [Medium](https://medium.com/@laila.kassam/mmm-bacon-the-engineering-of-consent-872e4476efd2)
- **Why it matters:** Bernays moved image/symbol-control from religion and the state into corporate and commercial mass persuasion — the deliberate, "scientific" use of symbols and imagery to shape mass behavior, often invisibly.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Edward Bernays, Sigmund Freud, Walter Lippmann; "Torches of Freedom"; the cigarette as symbol.
- **Sources:**

### 1875–1888 — Blavatsky and Theosophy transmit an esoteric symbol-system (publication events)
- **What happened:** Helena Petrovna Blavatsky co-founded the Theosophical Society in New York City on 17 November 1875 with Henry Steel Olcott and William Quan Judge. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helena_Blavatsky) She published *Isis Unveiled* in 1877 (its first 1,000-copy print run sold out in two days) and her major work *The Secret Doctrine* in two volumes in 1888 (Vol. 1, Cosmogenesis, 1 November; Vol. 2, Anthropogenesis, 28 December). She presented Theosophy as "the synthesis of science, religion and philosophy," claiming to revive an "Ancient Wisdom" underlying all religions, with extensive interpretation of comparative religious symbolism. (Scholars note *Isis Unveiled* drew heavily on other occult sources.)
- **Why it matters:** Theosophy systematized and popularized the *theory* that the world's religious symbols share a hidden, unified esoteric meaning — a key transmission node between Enlightenment astrotheology and 20th-century symbol-decoding. These are Blavatsky's interpretive claims; the factual content is the founding of the Society and the publication of her books.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** H.P. Blavatsky, Henry Steel Olcott; the Theosophical Society seal (ouroboros, swastika, Star of David, ankh, Om); the "Mahatmas."
- **Sources:**

### 1928 — Manly P. Hall compiles *The Secret Teachings of All Ages* (publication event)
- **What happened:** At age 27, Manly Palmer Hall (1901–1990) self-published *The Secret Teachings of All Ages: An Encyclopedic Outline of Masonic, Hermetic, Qabbalistic and Rosicrucian Symbolical Philosophy*, illustrated by J. Augustus Knapp. The first (Subscribers') edition was limited to 550 signed copies, printed in oversized format by H.S. Crocker Co., San Francisco. The work compiles and interprets symbolic traditions — Hermeticism, alchemy, Tarot, Pythagorean number, astrology, mythology, Freemasonry — arguing that "concealed within the emblematic figures, allegories and rituals of the ancients, is a secret doctrine." Hall founded the Philosophical Research Society in 1934.
- **Why it matters:** Hall's encyclopedia became the 20th century's most influential single compendium of esoteric symbol-interpretation, transmitting the astrotheology/hidden-symbolism tradition to later generations. Hall *compiled and interpreted* these symbol-systems; the factual claim is the publication and its influence, not the truth of the esoteric doctrine.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Manly P. Hall, J. Augustus Knapp; the pentagram, the Tarot, the All-Seeing Eye, Masonic and Rosicrucian symbols.
- **Sources:**
  1. TASCHEN / Philosophical Research Society, "Secret Teachings of All Ages" — https://www.prs.org/store/p2887/The_Secret_Teachings_of_All_Ages._TASCHEN_EDITION..html

### 1935 — Nazi spectacle (Riefenstahl) and Benjamin's diagnosis of aestheticized politics
- **What happened:** Leni Riefenstahl's *Triumph of the Will*, commissioned by Hitler and documenting the 1934 Nuremberg Nazi Party Congress, was released 28 March 1935; with 30 cameras and a crew of ~150, it turned a rally of hundreds of thousands into a choreographed spectacle portraying Hitler as a messianic figure. The same year, Walter Benjamin drafted "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction" (first version 1935), whose epilogue concludes: "The logical outcome of Fascism is an aestheticizing of political life. Communism responds by politicizing art." [Medium](https://medium.com/@orlorodriguez/from-theory-to-tragedy-rereading-benjamins-aestheticization-of-politics-and-politicization-of-f024929da094)
- **Why it matters:** The clearest 20th-century fusion of mass-reproduced imagery and total state power — and the canonical theoretical analysis (Benjamin) of how mechanical reproduction strips art of its "aura" and how fascism weaponizes aesthetics. Together they define the modern problem of image as political control.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Leni Riefenstahl, Adolf Hitler, Joseph Goebbels, Walter Benjamin; the swastika; the Nuremberg rally; "aura."
- **Sources:**
  1. Wikipedia, "The Work of Art in the Age of Mechanical Reproduction" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Work_of_Art_in_the_Age_of_Mechanical_Reproduction

### 1950s — Television advertising and the "engineering of consent" applied to TV
- **What happened:** As television entered most American homes in the 1950s, the persuasion techniques pioneered by Bernays and motivational researchers (e.g., Ernest Dichter) were applied to TV advertising and political broadcasting (such as the 1952 Eisenhower "Eisenhower Answers America" spots). Mass broadcast created a shared daily ritual of commercially controlled imagery reaching tens of millions simultaneously.
- **Why it matters:** Broadcast TV concentrated image-influence in a few networks and advertisers, industrializing the manufacture of desire and political consent through moving images on a scale Bernays had only theorized.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Edward Bernays, Ernest Dichter; the TV commercial; the political spot ad; the "captive" living-room audience.

### 1967 — Guy Debord, *The Society of the Spectacle*
- **What happened:** French Marxist theorist and Situationist Guy Debord published *La Société du spectacle* in 1967 (Paris, Éditions Buchet-Chastel), comprising 221 numbered theses. Its central argument: in advanced capitalism, authentic lived life has been replaced by its representation in images. [Academia.edu](https://www.academia.edu/25779448/Locating_the_Real_Guy_Debord_s_Society_of_the_Spectacle) Thesis 4: "The spectacle is not a collection of images, but a social relation among people, mediated by images." Debord traces a movement from *being* to *having* to *appearing*.
- **Why it matters:** The defining critical theory of the image-saturated consumer society — naming "the spectacle" as a system in which mediated images organize social life and pacify, anticipating the algorithmic-feed era by decades.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Guy Debord, the Situationist International; "the spectacle."
- **Sources:**

  2. Monoskop, Debord, *Society of the Spectacle* (1995 Zone Books ed.) — https://monoskop.org/images/e/e8/Debord_Guy_Society_of_the_Spectacle_1995.pdf

### 1990s–2000s — Astrotheology and symbol-decoding transmitted to mass/internet audiences
- **What happened:** A network of independent researchers and lecturers transmitted the Dupuis–Blavatsky–Hall astrotheology/symbol-decoding tradition to late-20th and early-21st-century audiences through books, video, radio, and the early internet. Jordan Maxwell (born Russell Joseph Pine, 1940–2022) popularized "astrotheology" and the decoding of religious and corporate/government symbols as solar-cult survivals; Michael Tsarion and the lecturer known as "Freeman" (Freeman Fly) advanced related symbol-decoding and "hidden ruler" narratives. This material fed directly into films like *Zeitgeist* (2007), which drew on the astrotheological Christ-myth thesis.
- **Why it matters:** This is the modern transmission node of the symbol-decoding worldview — the claim that elites communicate through hidden symbols embedded in religion, money, architecture, and media. These are the interpretive *teachings* of Maxwell, Tsarion, and Freeman, not established fact; what is documented is that they taught and published this material and influenced internet conspiracy culture.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Jordan Maxwell, Michael Tsarion, Freeman (Freeman Fly); the All-Seeing Eye, the obelisk, the zodiac; *Zeitgeist* (2007).

### 2004–2016 — Social media and the algorithmic feed
- **What happened:** Facebook launched in 2004; its News Feed launched 5 September 2006, shifting from static profiles to an aggregated stream and provoking immediate backlash (700,000+ users joined protest groups within days). The Like button (9 February 2009) introduced the core engagement metric; algorithmic ranking (EdgeRank, detailed 2010, retired ~2011) replaced chronological feeds with machine-ranked content. Instagram launched 6 October 2010 and adopted an algorithmic (non-chronological) feed in 2016; Twitter did likewise in 2016. TikTok (international launch 2017–2018) popularized the fully recommender-driven "For You" feed.
- **Why it matters:** Algorithmic feeds shifted control over which images and symbols people see from editors to engagement-optimizing recommender systems — a new, automated regime of image-curation that privileges whatever maximizes attention, with major implications for what symbols and narratives spread.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** Mark Zuckerberg, Facebook/Meta, Instagram, TikTok; the News Feed; the Like button; EdgeRank; the "For You" page.

### 2017 onward — Deepfakes
- **What happened:** In late 2017, a Reddit user with the screen name "deepfakes" used machine learning to swap celebrity faces into pornographic videos; journalist Samantha Cole reported it in a Vice/Motherboard article on 11 December 2017. The technique (built on Ian Goodfellow's 2014 Generative Adversarial Networks) gave the term its name ("deep learning" + "fake"). Deepfakes rapidly moved into fraud, non-consensual pornography, and political disinformation — culminating in a January 2024 AI-cloned robocall of Joe Biden's voice in the New Hampshire primary (see AI nodes below).
- **Why it matters:** Deepfakes break the long-standing evidentiary trust in photographic and video images — the most direct modern attack on the image as a reliable representation of reality, weaponizing synthetic imagery for deception.
- **Linked figures/symbols:** The Reddit user "deepfakes"; Ian Goodfellow (GANs); face-swap; synthetic video.

### 2022–2026 — AI image generation and the concentration of "who generates the culture's images"
- **What happened — the launch timeline:**
  - **OpenAI DALL·E** revealed 5 January 2021; **DALL·E 2** announced 6 April 2022 and opened to full public access (waitlist removed) 28 September 2022; [Tokenized](https://tokenizedhq.com/dall-e-2-release-date/) **DALL·E 3** released into ChatGPT October 2023.
  - **Midjourney** launched its open beta 12 July 2022 (via Discord), led by David Holz. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midjourney)
  - **Stable Diffusion** released publicly 22 August 2022 by the CompVis group (LMU Munich) and Runway, with Stability AI compute, under an open license.
  - **Adobe Firefly** first beta March 2023.
  - **Google** productized Imagen and later **Gemini image generation**; its "Nano Banana" (Gemini 2.5 Flash Image) launched in 2025 and drove a massive surge in image generation.
  - **Black Forest Labs** released **FLUX.1** on 1 August 2024; the company was founded in 2024 in Freiburg, Germany, by Robin Rombach, Andreas Blattmann, and Patrick Esser — the original creators of Stable Diffusion.
  - **xAI (Elon Musk)** integrated FLUX in August 2024 and announced its own model **Aurora** on 10 December 2024.
  - **OpenAI's GPT-4o native image generation (GPT Image 1)** launched 25 March 2025, immediately driving the viral "Studio Ghibli" trend. Per TechCrunch ("ChatGPT users have generated over 700M images since last week"), OpenAI COO Brad Lightcap said on 3 April 2025: "It's been a crazy first week for ChatGPT images… Since last Tuesday [26 March], over 130 million users have generated over 700 million images."
- **Who controls the models (2026):** Frontier image generation is concentrated in a handful of firms. OpenAI restructured on 28 October 2025 into a public benefit corporation; per CNBC (28 Oct 2025), Microsoft "now holds an investment in the PBC that is valued at $135 billion, or roughly 27% of the company on an as-converted diluted basis," with the OpenAI Foundation holding 26% and employees/investors the remaining 47%. Google DeepMind (Alphabet) controls Imagen/Gemini/Nano Banana and the SynthID watermarking system. Adobe (public) runs Firefly and now also hosts partner models. Stability AI (London) lost founder-CEO Emad Mostaque in March 2024. Midjourney remains independent and reportedly profitable with no traditional VC funding. Black Forest Labs is backed by Andreessen Horowitz and others. xAI is Musk's, integrated with X. Notably, the key researchers (Stable Diffusion's creators, now at Black Forest Labs) trace to one small academic lineage at LMU Munich.
- **Documented control concerns:**
  - **Deepfakes in elections:** an AI-cloned Biden robocall hit New Hampshire ~21 January 2024 (two days before the primary); the FCC ruled AI voices in robocalls illegal (February 2024), proposed a $6 million fine on consultant Steve Kramer, and fined transmitter Lingo Telecom $1 million; Kramer was acquitted by a jury in July 2025.
  - **Content provenance / watermarking:** Adobe founded the Content Authenticity Initiative in 2019; the **C2PA** (Coalition for Content Provenance and Authenticity) was founded in February 2021 by Adobe, Arm, BBC, Intel, Microsoft, and Truepic, [C2PA Viewer](https://c2paviewer.com/articles/what-is-c2pa) with a steering committee now including Google, Meta, OpenAI, Sony, and Amazon. [Defense](https://media.defense.gov/2025/Jan/29/2003634788/-1/-1/0/CSI-CONTENT-CREDENTIALS.PDF) Google's **SynthID** launched August 2023 (embedding imperceptible pixel watermarks) [Getclarity](https://www.getclarity.ai/ai-deepfake-blog/google-unveils-synthid-for-watermarking-ai-images) and expanded to text, video, and audio in 2024.
  - **Regulation:** The **EU AI Act** (Regulation 2024/1689) Article 50 requires generative-AI outputs to be machine-readably marked and deepfakes disclosed, with obligations applying from 2 August 2026; the European Commission published draft Codes of Practice on AI-content labeling in December 2025 and March 2026, naming C2PA Content Credentials and SynthID as example solutions. [C2PA Viewer](https://c2paviewer.com/articles/eu-ai-act-content-credentials)
  - **Copyright / training-data disputes:** *Andersen et al. v. Stability AI, Midjourney, DeviantArt* (artists' class action, filed 13 January 2023; key claims allowed to proceed in an August 2024 order); [Copyright Alliance](https://copyrightalliance.org/andersen-v-stability-ai-copyright-case/) *Getty Images v. Stability AI* (UK judgment 4 November 2025 largely rejected the copyright claims but found limited trademark infringement; a parallel US case continues); *The New York Times v. Microsoft & OpenAI* (filed 27 December 2023; most claims allowed to proceed in an April 2025 ruling).
  - **Demographic / representational bias:** A June 2023 Bloomberg analysis by Leonardo Nicoletti and Dina Bass ("Generative AI Takes Stereotypes and Bias From Bad to Worse") generated 5,100 images with Stable Diffusion v1.5 (300 per category, December 2022–February 2023, using the prompt "A color photograph of ____, headshot, high-quality") and found the model amplified race and gender stereotypes ("run by White male CEOs"). Google paused Gemini's image generation of people on 22 February 2024 after it produced historically inaccurate images [Aifrontierist](https://www.aifrontierist.com/google-gemini-ai-highlights-bias-issues/) (e.g., racially diverse Nazi-era soldiers and US Founding Fathers); SVP Prabhakar Raghavan said the feature "missed the mark."
- **Why it matters:** For the first time, the capacity to generate photorealistic images and symbols on demand — the culture's visual output — is concentrated in a few private firms whose models encode their training data's biases and whose outputs are increasingly indistinguishable from real photographs. This re-poses every historical image-control question (who may make images, which images are legitimate, how images are authenticated and policed) in an automated, planetary-scale form. The control responses (watermarking, provenance standards, the EU AI Act, copyright litigation) are the 2020s analogue of the councils, injunctions, and decrees of earlier eras.
