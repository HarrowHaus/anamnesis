# Citable Source Material for Five Pillar Essays: Images, Symbols, Propaganda, and Visual Literacy

## TL;DR
- All five pillars are now anchored with working links and tiered sources: hard Tier A scholarship and primary texts exist for P1, P2, and P4, whereas P3 (astrotheology) rests overwhelmingly on Tier B proponent works with only *adjacent* Tier A scholarship (solar-myth/Christ-myth historiography) — a genuine evidentiary gap that is flagged.
- The strongest primary-text anchors are public-domain or institutional and stable: Perseus (Plato), Project Gutenberg (Volney, Lippmann), Internet Archive (Bernays, Massey, Kuhn, Dupuis, Eire), and Cambridge/SVS Press (Brubaker–Haldon, John of Damascus).
- P5 is intentionally weighted to 2024–2026 sources (EU AI Act Article 50, C2PA Content Credentials, SynthID, the AI-image market) with named, dated reports; one preprint and one trade analysis are flagged as lower-tier.

## Key Findings
- Plato's *Republic* is best cited from Perseus (Shorey's Greek+English) for the primary passages, with the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy and Stephen Halliwell as the Tier A scholarly authorities on mimēsis and the modes.
- The "war on images" pillar has unusually deep Tier A scholarship: Mettinger on biblical aniconism, Brubaker–Haldon on Byzantine iconoclasm, and Eire on Reformation iconoclasm — each pairable with a primary theological text.
- Astrotheology has thin-to-nonexistent peer-reviewed coverage of the *movement* itself; the defensible Tier A move is to cite the historiography of solar mythology (Dorson, Carroll) and treat Dupuis/Volney/Massey/Kuhn as Tier B primary proponents.
- The Vicary subliminal episode must be cited as a documented, self-admitted fabrication — famous precisely because it was debunked — not as evidence that subliminal advertising works.
- AI-image provenance and regulation are fast-moving; cite official/primary sources (European Commission, NSA/CISA, the SynthID arXiv paper) and date every claim, because timelines are shifting (e.g., the EU AI Omnibus grandfathering rule).

### P1 — Who Controls the Image: Plato's *Republic* on Censorship and Mimesis
- Anchor sources:
1. Plato, *Republic* (Greek + English, trans. Paul Shorey), Perseus Digital Library, 1969 ed. — http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus:text:1999.01.0168 — Side-by-side Greek and English of the full *Republic*; supports direct citation of Book III (398–403, the banning of certain modes/harmonies) and Book X (the "three beds"/twice-removed mimēsis argument). **Tier A (primary text).**
2. Plato, *Republic* Book 3, page 398 (Perseus, Shorey) — http://www.perseus.tufts.edu/hopper/text?doc=Perseus%3Atext%3A1999.01.0168%3Abook%3D3%3Apage%3D398 — Pinpoints the mousikē/modes passage (retaining the Dorian and Phrygian modes, banning the "lamenting" and "soft/relaxed" modes). **Tier A (primary text).**
3. Scaife Viewer, Plato *Republic* (next-generation Perseus) — https://scaife.perseus.org/library/urn:cts:greekLit:tlg0059.tlg030/ — Updated reading environment hosting the same canonical text and translations. **Tier A (primary text).**
4. "Plato's Aesthetics," *Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy* — https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato-aesthetics/ — Authoritative survey of mimēsis in Books III and X; explicitly notes that whether the two books' accounts are compatible "remains the most controversial question about Plato's aesthetics." **Tier A.**
5. Stephen Halliwell, *The Aesthetics of Mimesis: Ancient Texts and Modern Problems* (Princeton UP, 2002); also *Plato Republic Book 10*, with introduction, translation and commentary (Aris & Phillips, 1988) — bibliographic record in the SEP entry above (https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato-aesthetics/) — The standard monograph and the standard Book X commentary on mimēsis. **Tier A.**
6. "Music and Education of the Soul in Plato and Aristotle" (HAL-SHS, open access) — https://shs.hal.science/halshs-00239672/document — Scholarly treatment of the modes (Dorian/Phrygian retained) across *Republic* III and *Politics* VIII. **Tier A.**
- Note: A respected modern complete translation is Plato, *Complete Works*, ed. John M. Cooper (Hackett, 1997), which includes the Grube/Reeve *Republic*; this is in copyright and has no public-domain link — cite the print edition.

### P2 — The War on Images: Aniconism and Iconoclasm
- Anchor sources:
1. **Exodus 20:4–6 / Deuteronomy 5:8–10** (the Second Commandment) — primary scriptural text; widely available, with a concise scholarly framing in Britannica's overview — https://www.britannica.com/topic/aniconism — Supports the Hebrew aniconic prohibition on graven images. **Tier A (primary text) / Tier C (reference frame).**
2. Tryggve N. D. Mettinger, *No Graven Image? Israelite Aniconism in Its Ancient Near Eastern Context* (Coniectanea Biblica OT 42, 1995) — review/record at https://www.journals.uchicago.edu/doi/10.1086/468681 (*Journal of Near Eastern Studies*) — The foundational scholarly study distinguishing "de facto" from "programmatic" aniconism. [AbeBooks](https://www.abebooks.com/9781575068213/Graven-Image-Israelite-Aniconism-Ancient-1575068214/plp) **Tier A.**
3. Leslie Brubaker & John Haldon, *Byzantium in the Iconoclast Era c. 680–850: A History* (Cambridge UP, 2011) — https://books.google.com/books/about/Byzantium_in_the_Iconoclast_Era_C_680_85.html?id=saTPtrgLTQoC — The definitive revisionist history of Byzantine iconoclasm. **Tier A.**
4. John of Damascus, *Three Treatises on the Divine Images* (trans. Andrew Louth, SVS Press, 2003) — https://archive.org/details/threetreatiseson0000stjo — The key primary defense of icons (the incarnational argument: "now that God has been seen in the flesh… I depict what I have seen of God"). **Tier A (primary text).**
5. **Second Council of Nicaea (787)** — primary conciliar acts; controversy documented at https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_Treatises_on_the_Divine_Images — Supports the iconophile resolution of 787. **Tier C (reference).** Flag: for citation, locate the full conciliar acts in a scholarly edition (e.g., the Sahas or Price translations of the acts of Nicaea II).
6. Carlos M. N. Eire, *War Against the Idols: The Reformation of Worship from Erasmus to Calvin* (Cambridge UP, 1986) — https://archive.org/details/WarAgainstTheIdolsTheReformationOfWorshipFromErasmusToCalvin — The standard study of Reformation iconoclasm (Karlstadt, Zwingli, Calvin, the *Beeldenstorm*). **Tier A.**
- Note: For **Islamic aniconism**, the strongest *directly verified* anchor located here is Britannica's aniconism entry (**Tier C**). A dedicated Tier A treatment (the *Encyclopaedia of Islam* article on *taswir*, or recent academic work such as Finbarr Barry Flood's scholarship on Islamic image practice) was not directly verified in this pass — **UNVERIFIED, flag for follow-up.**

- Anchor sources (Tier B proponents):

2. 3. Gerald Massey, *Ancient Egypt: The Light of the World* (1907) — https://archive.org/details/ancient-egypt-the-light-of-the-world_202407 — Massey's comparative Egyptian-origin thesis on religion and "the Jesus myth." **Tier B (proponent primary text).**
4. Alvin Boyd Kuhn, *The Lost Light: An Interpretation of Ancient Scriptures* — https://archive.org/details/lostlightaninter029017mbp — Kuhn's astro-allegorical reading of scripture. **Tier B (proponent primary text).**
- Tier A academic treatment of the underlying tradition:
5. Richard M. Dorson, "The Eclipse of Solar Mythology," *Journal of American Folklore* 68 (1955): 393–416 — cited/discussed in https://www.um.edu.mt/library/oar/bitstream/123456789/42903/1/Antae,_6(1)_-_A1.pdf — The classic account of how academic solar mythology collapsed under anthropological critique. **Tier A.**
6. Michael P. Carroll, "Some Third Thoughts on Max Müller and Solar Mythology," *European Journal of Sociology* 26 (1985): 263–281 — https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/european-journal-of-sociology-archives-europeennes-de-sociologie/article/abs/some-third-thoughts-on-max-muller-and-solar-mythology/B433CA017BC06A67BC66B6BA5043A760 — Critical scholarly reassessment of the solar-mythology school. **Tier A.**
- **Note (important):** Peer-reviewed academic coverage of "astrotheology" as a *contemporary movement* (Santos Bonacci's Universal Truth School, Jordan Maxwell) is thin to nonexistent; their materials remain Tier B / self-published proponent sources and links could not be verified here as stable scholarly resources — **UNVERIFIED for those two figures.** The Tier A scholarship above addresses the historical solar-myth/comparative-religion tradition the movement draws on, *not* the movement itself. This is a real evidentiary gap, not an oversight.

### P4 — The Engineering of Consent: Propaganda and Advertising Psychology
- Anchor sources:
1. Edward Bernays, *Propaganda* (1928) — https://archive.org/details/bernays-edward-l.-propaganda-1928-1936_202107 — The primary text opening: "The conscious and intelligent manipulation of the organized habits and opinions of the masses is an important element in democratic society." **Tier B (proponent primary text).** (Bernays's *Crystallizing Public Opinion*, 1923, and "The Engineering of Consent," 1947, are companion proponent works.)
2. Walter Lippmann, *Public Opinion* (1922) — https://www.gutenberg.org/ebooks/6456 — Foundational primary text on stereotypes, the "pseudo-environment," and what Lippmann/Bernays framed as the manufacture of consent. **Tier A / B (foundational primary text).**
3. Roland Barthes, *Mythologies* (1957) — primary text on the semiotics of advertising and myth as second-order signification. The English translation (Hill & Wang / Vintage) is in copyright; **UNVERIFIED public-domain link — cite the print edition.** **Tier A / B (primary text).**
4. **James Vicary subliminal episode (1957)** — Wikipedia, "James Vicary" — https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Vicary — Documents BOTH the original claim and its retraction: at a September 1957 press conference Vicary claimed "45,699 people were exposed to 1/3,000-second subliminal projections" telling them to "Eat Popcorn" and "Drink Coca-Cola," "causing a 57.5 percent sales increase for popcorn and an 18.1 percent increase in Coca-Cola sales"; in a **1962 *Advertising Age* interview (with Fred Danzig) Vicary admitted the study was "a gimmick" and the data "too small to be meaningful."** Independent investigators later found the Fort Lee theater manager had no record the six-week test ever occurred. **Tier C (reference; reliable for the documented fact of the fabrication).**
5. Robert Cialdini, *Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion* (1984) — modern persuasion scholarship; in copyright, cite print. **Tier A / B. UNVERIFIED public-domain link.**
- Note: Stuart Ewen (*Captains of Consciousness*, 1976; *PR!*, 1996) and Tim Wu (*The Attention Merchants*, 2016) are strong additional Tier B/C sources; both in copyright.

## Recommendations
1. **Build the spine from Tier A primary texts** for P1, P2, and P4 (Perseus Plato; Mettinger/Brubaker–Haldon/Eire + John of Damascus; Lippmann/Bernays), and lean on the SEP and named monographs (Halliwell) for scholarly authority. These pillars are publication-ready now.
2. **For P3, explicitly tier the sources in the essay itself:** present Dupuis/Volney/Massey/Kuhn as Tier B proponents and pair them with Tier A solar-myth historiography (Dorson, Carroll). Do not imply astrotheology is a peer-reviewed consensus, and state plainly that the contemporary movement (Bonacci, Maxwell) lacks academic treatment.
3. **For P4, always frame the Vicary episode as a debunked, self-admitted hoax** (cite the 1962 *Advertising Age* admission), using it as a case study in how a myth about image-power persists despite being false.
4. **For P5, date every claim and prioritize primary/official sources** (European Commission, NSA/CISA, arXiv SynthID). Where you currently rely on trade-blog market roundups (source 1), replace or corroborate with company announcements or filings before publication.
5. **Close the three flagged gaps before final publication:** (a) a Tier A Islamic-aniconism source (*Encyclopaedia of Islam* / Flood); (b) a scholarly edition of the Nicaea II acts; (c) a verified citation for any modern-astrotheology proponent you choose to quote.
- **Benchmarks that would change these recommendations:** if a peer-reviewed study of the modern astrotheology movement appears, upgrade P3 from "thin" to citable; if the EU AI Omnibus formally adopts the 2 December 2026 marking deadline, update all P5 dates accordingly; if the Roca et al. and *Journal of Business Research* papers complete peer review, upgrade them to firm Tier A.
