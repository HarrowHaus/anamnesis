## Terms (A–Z)


**Alchemy** — An ancient tradition of philosophy and proto-chemistry, practiced from Hellenistic Egypt through the Islamic world and Renaissance Europe, that sought to transform base metals into gold, produce an elixir of life, and (in its spiritual reading) perfect the practitioner's soul. Alchemy used an elaborate symbolic language and is regarded as a forerunner of modern chemistry as well as a major current of Western esotericism. | *Related:* Magnum Opus / Great Work, philosopher's stone, Hermeticism, occult | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/alchemy

**Allegory** — A symbolic narrative in which characters, events, or objects stand for abstract ideas, moral qualities, or hidden meanings, so the story can be read on more than one level. Allegory is central to esoteric symbolism because esoteric traditions often claim that myths and scriptures encode deeper teachings beneath their surface. | *Related:* mimesis, semiotics, esoteric/exoteric, archetype | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/art/allegory-art-and-literature

**Aniconism** — The religious or cultural avoidance of images depicting living creatures or the divine, found notably in Jewish, Islamic, and Byzantine traditions. It can range from mild discouragement of religious images to a strict prohibition, and when enforced through the physical destruction of images it becomes iconoclasm. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/aniconism) | *Related:* iconoclasm, semiotics | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/aniconism

**Anima / Animus** — In Carl Jung's analytical psychology, the unconscious contrasexual aspect of the psyche: the anima is the feminine side within a man and the animus the masculine side within a woman. [Encyclopedia.com](https://www.encyclopedia.com/psychology/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/animus-anima-analytical-psychology) Jung considered them archetypes that mediate between the conscious mind and the collective unconscious. | *Related:* archetype, collective unconscious | *Source:* https://www.encyclopedia.com/psychology/dictionaries-thesauruses-pictures-and-press-releases/animus-anima-analytical-psychology

**Archetype** — A primordial image, character, or pattern that recurs across myths, stories, and dreams consistently enough to seem universal. The term was popularized by the psychologist Carl Jung, who linked archetypes to a "collective unconscious" shared by all humans. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/archetype) | *Related:* collective unconscious, anima/animus, mandala, allegory | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/archetype

**"As above, so below"** — A widely quoted Hermetic axiom, paraphrasing a line from the Emerald Tablet, expressing the idea that the patterns of the higher cosmic realm are mirrored in the lower material realm (and the universe in the human being). It underpins the esoteric belief in correspondences between different levels of reality. | *Related:* microcosm/macrocosm, correspondences, Hermeticism, sacred geometry | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/As_above,_so_below

**Astrology** — A practice of divination that interprets the positions and movements of the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars as influencing or signaling earthly and human events. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/astrology) Practiced across many cultures since antiquity. | *Related:* zodiac, precession of the equinoxes, astrotheology, solstice/equinox | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/astrology

**Astrotheology** — The term has two distinct senses. In the context of esoteric symbolism it refers to the study of how the Sun, Moon, stars, and other celestial bodies shaped religious myths and symbols across ancient cultures. [Language & Humanities](https://www.languagehumanities.org/what-is-astrotheology.htm) (In modern academic theology it has a separate meaning: the study of the religious implications of astronomy and the possibility of extraterrestrial life.) [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrotheology) Some esoteric writers use astrotheology to argue that major religions are coded star-myths. | *Related:* astrology, zodiac, precession of the equinoxes, syncretism | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrotheology

**Axis mundi** — The "world axis" or cosmic center: a point or structure imagined as connecting heaven and earth, where the cardinal directions meet. It appears in many cultures as a sacred mountain, pillar, ladder, or world tree. | *Related:* World Tree, mandala, microcosm/macrocosm | *Source:* https://www.encyclopedia.com/environment/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/axis-mundi

**Caduceus** — The staff of the Greek god Hermes (Roman Mercury), depicted as a winged rod entwined by two serpents, symbolizing commerce, negotiation, and the crossing of boundaries. Because it resembles the single-snake Rod of Asclepius, it is sometimes used (especially in the United States) as a medical emblem. | *Related:* Hermeticism, ouroboros, occult | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/caduceus

**Collective unconscious** — Carl Jung's term for a layer of the unconscious shared by all humanity, distinct from the personal unconscious and said to contain the archetypes—universal images and patterns. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/science/collective-unconscious) It is a central concept for psychological readings of myth and symbol. | *Related:* archetype, anima/animus | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/science/collective-unconscious

**Correspondences (doctrine of)** — The esoteric idea that different planes of existence are systematically linked, so that things in the material world symbolically reflect and connect to higher spiritual realities. The concept was elaborated by the theologian Emanuel Swedenborg and is foundational to Western occult and Hermetic thought. | *Related:* "as above, so below," microcosm/macrocosm, Hermeticism, sacred geometry | *Source:* https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/encyclopedias-almanacs-transcripts-and-maps/correspondences-doctrine

**Demiurge** — From a Greek word for "craftsman," a creator figure who fashions the physical universe. In Plato's philosophy the Demiurge is a benevolent shaper of the cosmos; in many Gnostic systems it is reinterpreted as an ignorant or malevolent being who traps spirit in matter. | *Related:* gnosis/Gnosticism, Neoplatonism, Sophia, Logos | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demiurge

**Egregore** — A term in Western esotericism for a non-physical "thoughtform" or collective group-mind said to arise from and be sustained by the shared thoughts and emotions of a group. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egregore) It was popularized by 19th-century French occultists such as Éliphas Lévi. | *Related:* occult, occultism, theosophy | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egregore

**Esoteric / Exoteric** — A paired distinction: *esoteric* knowledge is inner, hidden, or reserved for initiates, while *exoteric* knowledge is outer, public, and accessible to everyone. Many religious and philosophical traditions are said to have both an outward, popular form and an inner, secret teaching. | *Related:* esotericism, occult, gnosis, mystery school, initiation | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/esotericism

**Esotericism (Western esotericism)** — An academic umbrella term for a range of Western traditions—such as Hermeticism, alchemy, astrology, Kabbalah, and occultism—that deal with hidden knowledge and were often marginalized by mainstream religion and science. Wouter J. Hanegraaff (Professor of History of Hermetic Philosophy, University of Amsterdam) framed the field this way in his 2012 Cambridge University Press monograph *Esotericism and the Academy: Rejected Knowledge in Western Culture*, describing the domain as traditions "dismissed as irrational or superstitious in Western society roughly since the period of the Enlightenment"; it is studied today with "methodological agnosticism" (without judging whether its claims are true). | *Related:* esoteric/exoteric, occultism, Hermeticism, gnosis, theosophy | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/esotericism

**The Engineering of Consent** — A phrase coined by public-relations pioneer Edward Bernays (nephew of Sigmund Freud), who defined it in his 1947 essay (in *The Annals of the American Academy of Political and Social Science*) as "the use of an engineering approach—that is, action based only on thorough knowledge of the situation and on the application of scientific principles and tried practices to the task of getting people to support ideas and programs." Bernays first introduced the idea in *Crystallizing Public Opinion* (1923) and later edited a 1955 book of the same title (University of Oklahoma Press), which opened with his 22-page chapter "The Theory and Practice of Public Relations: A Resumé" alongside chapters by associates, including his wife and partner Doris Fleischman. It is a foundational concept in the study of propaganda and persuasion, and is relevant to esoteric-symbolism studies because of how symbols are used persuasively in media and advertising. | *Related:* semiotics, mimesis, archetype | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Engineering_of_Consent

**Eye of Providence (All-Seeing Eye)** — A symbol of an eye, often within a triangle and surrounded by rays of light, representing the watchful eye of God or divine providence. Originating in Christian Trinitarian iconography, it later became associated with Freemasonry and appears on the reverse of the Great Seal of the United States. | *Related:* hexagram, sacred geometry, Freemasonry, occult | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eye_of_Providence

**Flower of Life** — A geometric figure of multiple evenly spaced, overlapping circles arranged in a sixfold symmetrical pattern, treated in the modern "sacred geometry" belief system as a symbol of creation and the interconnectedness of life. The motif appears decoratively in several ancient cultures. | *Related:* sacred geometry, vesica piscis, Platonic solids, golden ratio | *Source:* https://www.ancient-origins.net/human-origins-religions/ancient-secrets-lie-within-flower-life-001078

**Freemasonry** — The world's largest fraternal/secret society, which evolved from medieval stonemasons' guilds into symbolic ("speculative") lodges, with the first Grand Lodge founded in London in 1717. [The Archive](https://explorethearchive.com/what-is-freemasonry) It uses the tools and imagery of stonemasonry as allegories for moral and ethical instruction, and members advance through ceremonial degrees. [Bulwer1068](https://www.bulwer1068.org.uk/what-is-freemasonary) | *Related:* initiation, mystery school, Eye of Providence, Rosicrucianism, occult | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/Freemasonry

**Gematria** — A traditional method, especially in Kabbalah, of substituting numbers for the letters of the Hebrew alphabet and drawing interpretive connections between words whose letters add up to the same value. It is used to uncover supposed hidden meanings in sacred texts. | *Related:* Kabbalah, numerology, Tetragrammaton | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/gematria

**Gnosis / Gnosticism** — *Gnosis* is the Greek word for knowledge, specifically a saving, revealed insight into the divine. *Gnosticism* is a modern label for diverse religious movements of the early centuries CE that taught that a divine spark is trapped in the material world and can be freed through gnosis; many distinguished a flawed creator (the Demiurge) from a higher true God. | *Related:* Demiurge, Sophia, Hermeticism, esotericism, mysticism | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/gnosticism

**Golden ratio (phi)** — An irrational number, φ = (1 + √5)/2 ≈ 1.6180339887, which arises when a line is divided so that the whole is to the larger part as the larger part is to the smaller. As Encyclopædia Britannica puts it, it is "an irrational number often denoted by the Greek letter ϕ or τ, which is approximately equal to 1.618… the ratio of a line segment cut into two pieces of different lengths." It appears in geometry (such as the pentagram) and has long been associated with aesthetic harmony and "divine proportion." | *Related:* sacred geometry, Platonic solids, vesica piscis, Flower of Life | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/science/golden-ratio

**The Great Work (Magnum Opus)** — In alchemy, the full process of creating the philosopher's stone, often described through a sequence of color stages (blackening, whitening, yellowing, reddening). It is read both literally (a laboratory operation) and symbolically (the spiritual transformation of the practitioner). | *Related:* alchemy, philosopher's stone, Hermeticism, initiation | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnum_opus_(alchemy)

**Hermeticism** — A philosophical and religious tradition based on writings attributed to the legendary sage Hermes Trismegistus (the Hermetica), produced in Greco-Roman Egypt. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermeticism) It teaches the unity of the cosmos and the possibility of spiritual ascent through hidden knowledge, and has deeply influenced Western esotericism, alchemy, and astrology. | *Related:* gnosis, alchemy, "as above, so below," esotericism, Neoplatonism | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/Hermetism

**Hexagram** — A six-pointed star formed by two interlaced equilateral triangles. Widely known as the Star of David, a symbol of Judaism, it was historically called the Seal of Solomon and is used in Kabbalistic and occult traditions, where the interlocking triangles often symbolize the union of opposites. | *Related:* pentagram, sacred geometry, Kabbalah | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_of_David

**Hierophant** — Literally "one who displays sacred things"; originally the chief priest of the Eleusinian Mysteries who revealed the holy symbols during the rites. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/hierophant) By extension it means any interpreter of sacred mysteries; [TheFreeDictionary.com](https://www.thefreedictionary.com/hierophant) it is also a card in the Tarot's Major Arcana. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hierophant) | *Related:* mystery school, initiation, esoteric/exoteric, tarot | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/hierophant

**Iconoclasm** — The deliberate destruction of images or monuments, usually for religious or political reasons, often out of the belief that images are idolatrous or illegitimate. The most famous episodes occurred in the Byzantine Empire and during the Protestant Reformation. | *Related:* aniconism, semiotics | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/iconoclasm

**Initiation** — A ritual marking a person's transition into a new status or admission into a group, frequently structured around symbolic ordeals and themes of death and rebirth. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/initiation-rite) In esoteric traditions, initiation grants access to hidden teachings reserved for members. | *Related:* mystery school, hierophant, esoteric/exoteric, Freemasonry | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/initiation-rite

**Kabbalah (Qabalah)** — The principal tradition of Jewish mysticism, especially as developed from the 12th century onward, concerned with the nature of God (Ein Sof), creation, and the soul, and centered on the ten Sefirot and the Tree of Life. From the Renaissance, Christian and Hermetic adaptations (often spelled "Cabala" or "Qabalah") fed into Western occultism. | *Related:* gematria, Tetragrammaton, esotericism, theosophy | *Source:* https://www.encyclopedia.com/philosophy-and-religion/judaism/judaism/kabbalah

**Logos** — A Greek term meaning "word," "reason," or "underlying order." In Greek and Neoplatonic philosophy it denotes the rational ordering principle of the cosmos; in Christian theology it identifies Christ as the divine Word. | *Related:* Neoplatonism, Demiurge, Sophia, gnosis | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism

**Mandala** — In Hindu and Buddhist traditions, a symbolic geometric diagram representing the universe, used in ritual and as a focus for meditation. Carl Jung also studied mandalas as spontaneous symbols of psychological wholeness. | *Related:* sacred geometry, axis mundi, archetype, microcosm/macrocosm | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/mandala-diagram

**Microcosm / Macrocosm** — A Western philosophical schema in which the human being is a "little world" (microcosm) that mirrors the universe or "great world" (macrocosm). [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/microcosm) Rooted in ancient Greek thought, it was developed by Neoplatonists, Gnostics, Kabbalists, and Renaissance thinkers. | *Related:* "as above, so below," correspondences, axis mundi, sacred geometry | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/microcosm

**Mimesis** — A Greek term for imitation or representation, central to ancient theories of art. Plato distrusted mimesis as a mere copy of reality, while Aristotle saw it as a natural and instructive human activity; [Number Analytics](https://www.numberanalytics.com/blog/ultimate-guide-to-mimesis) the concept matters for symbolism because it concerns how images represent—or distort—what is real. | *Related:* semiotics, allegory, archetype, the engineering of consent | *Source:* https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/plato-aesthetics/

**Mysticism** — A constellation of practices and experiences aimed at direct union with, or experience of, ultimate reality or the divine. [Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mysticism/) The word derives from the Greek *mystes*, an initiate of a mystery cult. | *Related:* gnosis, esotericism, mystery school, theosophy | *Source:* https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/mysticism/

**Mystery school** — A secret religious institution of the ancient Greco-Roman world—most famously the Eleusinian Mysteries—in which initiates underwent staged ritual experiences to gain esoteric knowledge unavailable to outsiders. Mystery schools are often cited as organizational ancestors of later secret societies. | *Related:* initiation, hierophant, esoteric/exoteric, Freemasonry, mysticism | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/Eleusinian-Mysteries

**Neoplatonism** — A philosophical system developed from the 3rd century CE by Plotinus and his successors, holding that all reality emanates from a single ineffable source (the One) through levels including the divine Mind (Nous) and Soul. It strongly shaped Christian, Gnostic, Hermetic, and later esoteric thought. | *Related:* Demiurge, Logos, gnosis, theurgy, Hermeticism | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism

**Numerology** — The belief that numbers carry hidden meaning and can reveal character or foretell the future, often by assigning numerical values to the letters of a name or to a birth date. It draws on the Pythagorean idea that reality is ultimately structured by numbers. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/numerology) | *Related:* gematria, sacred geometry, golden ratio | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/numerology

**Occult / Occultism** — *Occult* comes from the Latin for "hidden" and refers to hidden forces or secret knowledge. *Occultism* is a group of esoteric movements emerging mainly in 19th-century Europe (associated with figures like Éliphas Lévi and later Helena Blavatsky) that sought to integrate older esoteric traditions—magic, astrology, alchemy, Kabbalah, tarot—with the modern world. | *Related:* esotericism, theosophy, Hermeticism, egregore | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/occultism

**Ouroboros** — An ancient emblem of a serpent or dragon devouring its own tail, expressing eternal cycles of destruction and renewal and the unity of all things. It is a recurring symbol in Gnostic and alchemical imagery. | *Related:* alchemy, the Great Work, gnosis | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/Ouroboros

**Pentagram / Pentacle** — A five-pointed star drawn in a single continuous line, used historically as a symbol of protection, the human figure, and the elements. When enclosed in a circle it is often called a pentacle and is especially associated with modern Neo-Pagan religions such as Wicca. | *Related:* hexagram, sacred geometry, golden ratio, occult | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/pentagram

**Philosopher's stone** — The legendary substance sought by alchemists, believed able to turn base metals into gold and, in some accounts, to cure disease and confer long life. It symbolized perfection and spiritual illumination, and its pursuit was called the Great Work. | *Related:* alchemy, the Great Work, Hermeticism | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosopher's_stone

**Platonic solids** — The five three-dimensional shapes whose faces are all identical regular polygons: the tetrahedron, cube, octahedron, dodecahedron, and icosahedron. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid) In Plato's *Timaeus* they were associated with the classical elements and the heavens, [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid) and they remain important in sacred geometry. | *Related:* sacred geometry, golden ratio, Flower of Life, microcosm/macrocosm | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/science/Platonic-solid

**Precession of the equinoxes** — The slow, cyclical wobble of Earth's axis of rotation, which causes the equinox points to drift westward against the background stars. Per Encyclopædia Britannica, it is "a cyclic wobbling in the orientation of Earth's axis of rotation with a period of 25,772 years," first noticed by the Greek astronomer Hipparchus in his star catalog completed in 129 BCE. It explains why the zodiac signs no longer align with their original constellations and features in astrotheological and astrological symbolism. | *Related:* zodiac, astrology, astrotheology, solstice/equinox | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/science/precession-of-the-equinoxes

**Rosicrucianism** — A spiritual movement, emerging in early-17th-century Europe through anonymous manifestos, that claimed access to ancient esoteric wisdom symbolized by a rose on a cross. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosicrucianism) It blends Hermeticism, Kabbalah, and Christian mysticism [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rosicrucians) and describes a likely fictional founder, Christian Rosenkreuz. | *Related:* Hermeticism, Freemasonry, occultism, esotericism | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/Rosicrucians

**Sacred geometry** — The belief that certain shapes and proportions carry spiritual or symbolic meaning and reflect the underlying order of the universe. The term was popularized in the 20th century; it draws on genuine mathematics like the golden ratio and Platonic solids. | *Related:* golden ratio, Platonic solids, Flower of Life, vesica piscis, mandala | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sacred_geometry

**Semiotics** — The study of signs and symbols and how they convey meaning. Founded by Ferdinand de Saussure and Charles Sanders Peirce, it analyzes how something (a word, image, or object) can stand for something else, and is widely applied to media, art, and culture. | *Related:* mimesis, the engineering of consent, allegory, aniconism | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/science/semiotics

**Sigil** — A symbol believed to hold magical power; traditionally a "seal" representing a spirit in medieval grimoires, and in modern magic (especially chaos magic) a glyph created by condensing the letters of a desired intention. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigil) | *Related:* talisman, occult, correspondences | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigil

**Solstice / Equinox** — The solstices are the two moments each year when the Sun reaches its farthest point north or south, producing the longest and shortest days (around June 21 and December 21). The equinoxes are the two moments when day and night are nearly equal (around March 21 and September 23). Both result from Earth's axial tilt and have long been marked in religious and symbolic calendars. | *Related:* precession of the equinoxes, zodiac, astrology, astrotheology | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/science/solstice

**Sophia** — A Greek word for "wisdom," personified in Gnostic and Neoplatonic thought as a divine feminine figure. In many Gnostic myths, Sophia's "fall" leads to the creation of the material world and the emergence of the Demiurge. | *Related:* gnosis/Gnosticism, Demiurge, Logos, theosophy | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demiurge

**Syncretism** — The blending or fusion of different religious or philosophical beliefs and practices into a new combined system. [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-syncretism) It was especially prominent in the Hellenistic world [Encyclopedia Britannica](https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-syncretism) and is important for understanding how esoteric symbols migrate across traditions. | *Related:* Hermeticism, gnosis, astrotheology, theosophy | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/religious-syncretism

**Talisman** — An object, often engraved with a symbol or character, believed to attract good fortune or protective power. It is closely related to the amulet, which is usually described as warding off harm. | *Related:* sigil, correspondences, occult | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/talisman

**Tarot** — A deck of 78 cards (22 Major Arcana "trumps" plus four suits) invented in 15th-century northern Italy for card games. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarot) From the late 18th century, especially through French occultists, it became widely used for divination, with each card assigned symbolic meanings; scholars find no evidence of divinatory use before the late 1700s. [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarot) | *Related:* hierophant, occultism, numerology, archetype | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/tarot

**Tetragrammaton** — The four-letter Hebrew name of God (YHWH), considered too sacred to pronounce in Judaism. Its letters and numerical values are central to Kabbalistic interpretation and feature heavily in Western ceremonial magic. | *Related:* Kabbalah, gematria, occultism | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetragrammaton

**Theosophy** — In its modern sense, a religious movement founded in 1875 by Helena Blavatsky and others, drawing on Neoplatonism, Hinduism, and Buddhism and teaching an "ancient wisdom" underlying all religions. (In an older sense, "theosophy" also names mystical systems such as that of Jakob Böhme.) Theosophy strongly shaped 20th-century occult and New Age movements. | *Related:* occultism, esotericism, Kabbalah, mysticism | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/theosophy

**Theurgy** — From the Greek for "divine working," a set of ritual practices used by Neoplatonist philosophers such as Iamblichus to invoke the presence of the gods and elevate the soul toward the divine. It contrasts with purely intellectual contemplation. | *Related:* Neoplatonism, mysticism, occult, initiation | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoplatonism

**Vesica piscis** — A lens- or almond-shaped figure formed where two equal circles overlap, each passing through the other's center. It is a foundational form in sacred geometry, associated with creation and unity and serving as the basis for figures such as the Flower of Life. | *Related:* sacred geometry, Flower of Life, golden ratio, Platonic solids | *Source:* https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesica_piscis

**World Tree** — A mythological cosmic tree whose trunk stands at the center of the universe and whose branches and roots connect the various realms of existence; the Norse Yggdrasil is the best-known example. It is closely related to the "tree of life" motif and the idea of an axis mundi. | *Related:* axis mundi, microcosm/macrocosm, Kabbalah | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/Yggdrasill

**Zodiac** — A band of (traditionally twelve) constellations around the sky through which the Sun, Moon, and planets appear to move. Divided by the Babylonians into twelve 30° signs, it forms the basis of astrological interpretation, though precession means the signs no longer match the constellations they were named for. | *Related:* astrology, precession of the equinoxes, solstice/equinox, astrotheology | *Source:* https://www.britannica.com/topic/zodiac
