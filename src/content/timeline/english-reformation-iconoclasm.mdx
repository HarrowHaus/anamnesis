English iconoclasm came in state-sanctioned waves: Thomas Cromwell's 1538 injunctions, the Royal Injunctions of 1547 ordering a wave of destruction, and a 1549 decree commanding citizens to "deface and destroy" images. A second major wave followed in the 1640s under Puritan authority, including William Dowsing's commissioned campaign through East Anglia. The characteristic method — striking off noses, hands, and faces from stone figures, then whitewashing painted walls — taught reformation through deliberate, visible absence.
