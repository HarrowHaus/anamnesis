Alois Senefelder invented lithography around 1796–1798, enabling cheap, high-volume printing of images without the laborious work of woodcut or metal engraving. Chromolithography extended the method to full color, fueling an explosion of mass-produced advertising posters, illustrated newspapers, and commercial iconography through the 19th century — particularly the large color advertising poster popularized in late-19th-century Paris. Images were no longer precious; they were everywhere, and they were for sale.
