## The surface

In 1951 CBS art director William Golden designed the network's new logo after being struck by a "hex eye" barn symbol reproduced in *Portfolio* magazine — a Pennsylvania-Dutch Shaker all-seeing-eye device painted to ward off evil. Artist Kurt Weihs executed the final version: one human eye inside a smooth oval, flat and stark, opening like a camera shutter. The design has anchored CBS's identity ever since.

## The decode

Jordan Maxwell reads the CBS eye as a modern survival of the **all-seeing eye** — the eye of the sun-god as emblem of watching, illumination, and control — placed at the centre of a broadcasting corporation that describes its own function as *broadcasting to the public*. In Maxwell's framework this is no accident of taste: the symbol signals what the network actually does, which is to watch and to be watched, to surveil the audience even as it entertains. The "Eye Network" names itself with the oldest symbol of divine oversight, hidden at the scale of the logo.

## The symbol lineage

The eye-in-form motif carries a long documented descent. The **eye-of-horus** — the solar-protective eye of the Egyptian god Horus/Ra — is among the oldest known apotropaic symbols, painted on coffins and amulets across millennia to signify divine sight and protection. It enters European iconography as the Eye of Providence: God's omniscience drawn inside a rayed triangle, appearing in Renaissance Christian art before being placed on the United States Great Seal in 1782. CBS's own William Golden reached the form through a direct American folk survival — the Shaker hex "all-seeing eye" painted on barns. Three lineages, one image: the solar eye of the ancient world, the providential eye of the new republic, and the corporate eye of the broadcast age fold into the same oval.
