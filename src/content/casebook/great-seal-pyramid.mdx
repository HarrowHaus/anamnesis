## The surface
On the reverse of the one-dollar bill stands a pyramid of thirteen courses, left unfinished at the top. Its base carries the Roman numerals **MDCCLXXVI** — 1776. Above the truncated summit floats a triangle wreathed in rays, and inside it, a single open eye. Two mottoes frame the scene: *Annuit Cœptis* above, *Novus Ordo Seclorum* below. The design was finalized for the Great Seal in 1782 and placed on the dollar in 1935.

## The decode
Manly P. Hall reads the seal as **"the signature of the Mysteries"** — the emblem, set openly on the national currency, of an initiatic tradition guiding the new republic. In his reading the unfinished pyramid is a Mason's *trestleboard*: the Great Work still in progress, the structure deliberately left open at the top. The radiant eye is the eye of the illumined order — the few who see, set above the many. And *Novus Ordo Seclorum*, "a new order of the ages," names the long objective Hall argues the founders encoded into the seal itself: "Careful analysis of the seal discloses a mass of occult and Masonic symbols."

## The symbol lineage
Each part of the seal is a Dictionary symbol with a documented descent. The **all-seeing eye** enters Western art as the Renaissance Eye of Providence — God's omniscience drawn as an eye in a rayed triangle — and is folded onto the seal in 1782. The **unfinished pyramid**, thirteen courses for the thirteen states, carries the older language of the monument left deliberately incomplete. The **eagle** of the seal's obverse, Hall notes, is a conventionalized **phoenix** — the solar bird of rebirth descended from the Egyptian Bennu of Heliopolis. Even the mottoes are inherited: *Annuit Cœptis* and *Novus Ordo Seclorum* both adapt lines of Virgil. Trace each element back and the seal resolves into a lineage of inherited symbols, assembled into one mark and printed a billion times over.
